How to avoid censorship

A Law That Doesn't Add Up

Requiring everyone — adults included — to verify their identity before using social media to address concerns about minors is a blunt, privacy-invasive solution to a narrow problem. Section 2(b) of the Charter protects freedom of expression, including the ability to access information and communicate anonymously, and a blanket verification mandate risks chilling that speech for millions while creating a tempting new database of sensitive personal data — without solving the underlying issue.

How to Find Your Member of Parliament

Finding the MP who represents your exact neighborhood is incredibly easy. All you need to do is go to the official Canadian House of Commons website at ourcommons.ca/members/en and look for the "Members of Parliament" search tool. Simply type your postal code into the search bar, and it will immediately pull up your local representative, along with their official government email address, office phone number, and mailing address. You can email them directly or send a physical letter to their Ottawa office postage-free.

Subject: Opposition to the Social Media Ban – Protecting Privacy and Charter Rights for Youth

Dear [MP's Name],

I am writing to you today as a constituent in your riding to express my strong opposition to the proposed ban on social media for youth under the Safe Social Media Act (Bill C-34). While I understand the government’s desire to improve online safety, a blanket ban is a heavy-handed approach that creates severe privacy risks for all Canadians and violates the fundamental rights of teenagers.

First, enforcing an age ban requires platforms to implement strict age verification systems. This will effectively eliminate online privacy, as social media companies will be forced to collect sensitive government IDs, passports, or use invasive biometric scanning just to verify who is allowed to log on. Forcing Canadians to hand over sensitive personal data to massive tech corporations creates massive cybersecurity risks and destroys our ability to communicate anonymously.

Second, banning teenagers from these platforms directly infringes upon their rights under Section 2(b) of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, which guarantees the fundamental freedom of thought, belief, opinion, and expression. For young people today, social media is the modern public square. It is the primary place where teenagers learn about global events, connect with their peers, and express their identities. Stripping youth of their access to these digital spaces suppresses their constitutional right to free expression and isolates them from the world.

I urge you to stand up for the privacy of all Canadians and the Charter rights of young people by opposing this ban.

Where Canada Currently Stands

Right now, the ban is not law yet, but the wheels are officially in motion. On June 10, 2026, the federal government (under Prime Minister Mark Carney and Culture Minister Marc Miller) introduced Bill C-34, the Safe Social Media Act. This is the government's latest attempt to create a legal framework for online safety, following previous failed attempts like 2024's Bill C-63. Because the current government has a majority, this bill has a much smoother path to passing Parliament, though it still has to go through the standard legislative process. The government has stated that setting up the necessary regulatory body to enforce the rules—the new Digital Safety Commission of Canada—could take up to 18 months after the bill officially becomes law.
