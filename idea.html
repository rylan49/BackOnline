exhibit 01 — the legislation

What Bill C-34 actually does

On June 10, 2026, the federal government tabled Bill C-34 — the Safe Social Media Act — as part of a broader Digital Safety Act. Here's what's actually in it, stripped of the press-release language.

who's behind it
Introduced by the Minister of Canadian Identity and Culture. Framed as a response to youth mental health harms and the wave of similar laws passed in Australia and elsewhere.
who's affected
Anyone under 16 in Canada would be barred from holding a social media account — full stop. Platforms could regain the right to offer accounts to under-16s only if they meet safety standards set by a new federal regulator.
new enforcement body
Creates a Digital Safety Commission of Canada, with the power to set standards, investigate platforms, and levy fines. It would also write new rules for AI chatbots.
penalties
Non-compliant platforms face fines of up to 3% of their global revenue, or $10 million CAD — whichever is larger.
timeline
Government officials estimate roughly a year for the bill to pass, plus another 18 months to actually stand up the regulator — meaning the rules that decide which platforms we're allowed to use could be written long after this bill becomes law.
the precedent
Australia's under-16 ban took effect on December 10, 2025. Within about a month, major platforms had deactivated roughly 4.7 million accounts belonging to people judged to be under the age limit — including plenty of people who were actually old enough, caught by imperfect age checks.
exhibit 02 — witness statements

What we'd actually lose

Lawmakers keep talking about "social media" like it's one thing — doomscrolling and influencer culture. For a lot of us, it's also the only place we found our people. These statements come from youth across Alberta. Tap a name to declassify it.

I'm the only trans kid in my school. The only reason I survived grade 9 was a Discord server full of other trans teens from across the country. If that counts as "social media" under this bill, I lose the only place I've never had to explain myself.

My school's climate club organizes entirely over Instagram — event pages, petition links, photos from our protests. If I can't have an account, I'm not just losing a feed, I'm losing my role in something I've worked on for two years.

I have a chronic illness and most of what I know about managing it came from other young patients posting about their own experience. My doctor's office doesn't talk to me like that. A 14-year-old in Ontario who's been through it does.

Nobody asked us. There were no 15-year-olds in the room when this bill was written. We're old enough to be the subject of national legislation but not old enough to be consulted on it.

The case against the ban

We're not saying social media is perfect — far from it. But a blanket ban on everyone under 16 is the wrong tool for the problem, and the evidence backs that up.

the government says A ban will protect kids from the harms of social media.
but Researchers who study this call a blanket ban a "sledgehammer approach" — it doesn't touch the actual design choices (autoplay, infinite scroll, algorithmic feeds) that drive harm for users of every age. It removes the platform instead of fixing what's broken about it.
the government says Platforms can simply verify who's old enough.
but Reliable age verification means everyone — adults included — hands over ID or biometric data to use basic apps. Privacy advocates have flagged this as one of the law's biggest open questions, and Australia's rollout shows how messy and error-prone it gets in practice.
the government says Kids will just find something healthier to do instead.
but Experts who study youth and tech have been blunt: bans don't deter young people who are determined to stay connected. They push toward unregulated apps, VPNs, and borrowed adult accounts — platforms with far less moderation than the ones being banned.
the government says This is just about "social media," not free expression.
but Even some of the bill's opponents in Parliament have warned that its broader Criminal Code provisions could chill speech well beyond what most people picture as "social media" — and for marginalized youth especially, these platforms are often the only place to find community at all.
